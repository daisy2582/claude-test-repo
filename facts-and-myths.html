Separating Truth from Fiction

Facts &
Myths

Misconceptions about veganism are everywhere. Let us debunk the most common myths with science-backed facts and help you make informed decisions.

Debunked

Common Myths About Veganism

Click each myth to reveal the truth

FACT

Plant-based foods are rich in protein. Lentils, chickpeas, tofu, tempeh, seitan, quinoa, nuts, and seeds all provide substantial protein. The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics confirms that well-planned vegan diets meet all protein needs at every stage of life, including for athletes. Many of the world's strongest athletes — including Olympic weightlifters and ultramarathon runners — thrive on plant-based diets.

FACT

The cheapest foods in the world are plant-based: rice, beans, lentils, potatoes, oats, pasta, and seasonal vegetables. A 2021 Oxford study found that vegan diets are up to 30% cheaper than meat-based diets in high-income countries. While specialty vegan products can be pricey, whole-food plant-based eating is among the most affordable ways to eat well.

FACT

Human anatomy is more similar to herbivores than carnivores — our long intestines, flat molars, and jaw movement are designed for grinding plants, not tearing flesh. While early humans were omnivores out of necessity, modern humans have no biological need for animal products. Every major dietetic association in the world confirms that a well-planned vegan diet is nutritionally adequate and may provide health benefits.

FACT

Plants do not have a central nervous system, brain, or pain receptors. While plants respond to stimuli (like sunlight), this is fundamentally different from the conscious experience of suffering that animals have. Even if plants could feel pain, a vegan diet uses far fewer plants overall — because it takes many more pounds of plants to feed animals than to feed humans directly. Going vegan reduces total plant deaths too.

FACT

One person going vegan saves approximately 200 animals per year, reduces their carbon footprint by 73%, saves 401,500 gallons of water, and prevents 7,300 pounds of CO2 emissions annually. Beyond personal impact, every vegan influences an average of 3 other people to reduce their animal product consumption. Social change starts with individual action — every movement began with one person making a choice.

FACT

Excellent plant-based calcium sources include kale, broccoli, bok choy, fortified plant milks, tofu made with calcium sulfate, almonds, and sesame seeds. For iron, lentils, chickpeas, spinach, quinoa, fortified cereals, and pumpkin seeds are all rich sources. Pairing iron-rich foods with vitamin C (like lemon juice on lentils) dramatically increases absorption. Studies show vegans have similar or better iron levels than omnivores.

FACT

Labels like "free-range," "cage-free," and "organic" are largely marketing terms with minimal legal requirements. "Free-range" chickens may still be confined to overcrowded barns with a small door to a tiny outdoor area. "Humane" certified operations still send animals to the same slaughterhouses. Male chicks are still killed in the egg industry, and dairy calves are still separated from their mothers — regardless of the label.

FACT

Over 77% of the world's soy is fed to livestock, not humans. Only 7% of soy is consumed directly by people (as tofu, tempeh, soy milk, etc.). The soy destroying the Amazon is grown for animal feed. If everyone went vegan, we would actually need far less soy — and could return vast areas of farmland to wildlife habitat. Eating plants directly is always more land-efficient than filtering them through animals.

FACT

Some of the world's most flavorful cuisines are plant-heavy: Indian curries, Thai stir-fries, Ethiopian stews, Mexican tacos, Mediterranean mezze, and Japanese noodle dishes. Going vegan often expands your palate because you discover new ingredients, spices, and cooking techniques. The plant-based food industry has also exploded with innovative products — from realistic burgers to artisan cheeses — that even omnivores love.

FACT

The American Academy of Pediatrics, British Dietetic Association, and many other health bodies confirm that well-planned vegan diets are suitable for all stages of life, including pregnancy, infancy, and childhood. The key is "well-planned" — ensuring adequate B12, vitamin D, omega-3s, and calories, which is true for any diet. Many vegan children thrive with excellent growth and development outcomes.
