Facts & Myths

Separating fiction from reality when it comes to veganism, nutrition, and animal agriculture.

Debunked

Common Myths About Veganism

Click each myth to reveal the facts

Fact

Plant-based foods like lentils, chickpeas, tofu, tempeh, quinoa, hemp seeds, and edamame are excellent protein sources. The American Dietetic Association confirms that well-planned vegan diets provide adequate protein for all life stages. Many elite athletes including Lewis Hamilton, Venus Williams, and Scott Jurek thrive on plant-based protein.

Fact

Vegan cuisine spans every world culture -- from Thai curries to Mexican tacos, Italian pasta to Indian dals. With thousands of plant ingredients, herbs, and spices, vegan cooking offers virtually unlimited flavor combinations. Many of the world's most beloved dishes -- hummus, falafel, pasta marinara, stir-fries -- are naturally vegan.

Fact

A 2021 Oxford study found that vegan diets are 30% cheaper in high-income countries. Staples like rice, beans, lentils, oats, pasta, and seasonal vegetables are among the most affordable foods globally. Specialty vegan products can be pricey, but a whole-food plant-based diet is consistently one of the most budget-friendly ways to eat.

Fact

Dark leafy greens (kale, broccoli, bok choy), fortified plant milks, tofu made with calcium sulfate, and almonds are all rich in calcium. Iron is abundant in lentils, chickpeas, spinach, quinoa, and pumpkin seeds. Pairing iron-rich foods with vitamin C (like lemon juice) significantly boosts absorption.

Fact

One person going vegan saves approximately 200 animals per year, conserves 401,500 gallons of water, 14,600 pounds of grain, 10,950 square feet of forest, and reduces CO2 emissions by 3,322 pounds annually. Every movement starts with individual choices -- collectively, vegans have already shifted markets, menus, and policies worldwide.

Fact

Human anatomy shares more traits with herbivores than carnivores -- we have flat molars for grinding, long intestines, and lack claws or sharp canines. Many traditional cultures thrived on predominantly plant-based diets. Modern factory farming bears no resemblance to our ancestors' relationship with food. Today we have the knowledge and resources to thrive on plants.

Fact

Over 77% of global soy is fed to livestock, not humans. Soy consumed directly (tofu, edamame, soy milk) is linked to reduced cancer risk and improved heart health. The phytoestrogens in soy are different from human estrogen and do not cause hormonal issues. If we stopped feeding soy to animals, deforestation for soy would plummet.

Fact

"Free-range" often means thousands of birds in a shed with a small outdoor access point most never use. "Organic" animals still face the same slaughter process. Labels like "humane" are largely unregulated marketing terms. The only way to ensure no animal suffers for your food is to choose plant-based alternatives.

Fact

The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics states that "appropriately planned vegan diets are healthful, nutritionally adequate, and may provide health benefits" for all life stages, including pregnancy, lactation, infancy, childhood, and adolescence. With attention to B12, iron, omega-3s, and vitamin D, vegan families thrive worldwide.

Fact

Plants lack a central nervous system, brain, and pain receptors. While plants respond to stimuli, this is fundamentally different from the conscious pain experience of animals. Furthermore, eating plants directly requires far fewer plants than feeding them to animals first -- livestock consume far more plants than humans would by eating plant-based directly.

Fact

B12 is produced by bacteria, not animals. Livestock are often supplemented with B12 themselves. Vegans can easily get B12 from fortified foods (plant milks, nutritional yeast, cereals) or a simple supplement. B12 deficiency is also common in meat-eaters over 50, making supplementation broadly recommended regardless of diet.

Fact

Farm animal populations are artificially maintained through forced breeding. A global shift to veganism would happen gradually, reducing demand and therefore breeding over time. The remaining animals would live out their lives in sanctuaries. We would not release billions of animals at once -- this is a gradual transition, not an overnight switch.
